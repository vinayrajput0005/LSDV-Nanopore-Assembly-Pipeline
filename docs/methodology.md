# Methodology

## Overview

The LSDV Nanopore Assembly Pipeline is a reproducible computational workflow developed for the assembly and characterization of Lumpy Skin Disease Virus (LSDV) genomes from Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) sequencing data. The pipeline integrates quality assessment, sequential host depletion, long-read genome assembly, consensus polishing, reference-guided scaffolding, assembly validation, and coverage analysis within a unified analytical framework.

The workflow was designed to support genomic surveillance, outbreak investigations, molecular epidemiology, and comparative genomic studies of LSDV. Particular emphasis was placed on minimizing non-target sequence interference, maximizing assembly continuity, and generating standardized quality metrics suitable for downstream comparative analyses.

---

## Input Data

The pipeline accepts Oxford Nanopore sequencing reads in compressed FASTQ format. Multiple samples can be processed in a single execution, with each sample analyzed independently through an identical computational workflow.

Input sequencing data are assumed to originate from high-accuracy Nanopore basecalling workflows and are supplied as gzipped FASTQ files. No prior quality filtering is performed before quality assessment and host depletion.

---

## Quality Assessment

Initial evaluation of sequencing performance is performed using NanoPlot. This step provides an overview of read-length distributions, sequencing yield, read quality scores, and read N50 statistics. Quality assessment is conducted primarily to facilitate dataset evaluation and does not alter the input reads used in downstream analyses.

Generated reports allow rapid identification of sequencing failures, unexpected read-length distributions, and reductions in sequencing yield that may influence assembly performance.

---

## Sequential Host and Contaminant Depletion

Removal of non-target sequences represents a critical component of the workflow. Viral sequencing datasets derived from clinical material or propagated viral isolates frequently contain host-derived nucleic acids and cell-culture contaminants that may negatively affect assembly accuracy.

To maximize specificity, reads are sequentially aligned against multiple reference databases using minimap2. After each alignment stage, mapped reads are removed and only unmapped reads are retained for subsequent analysis.

The first depletion stage targets human-derived sequences using the GRCh38 human reference genome. This step removes host contamination originating from clinical specimens and sample handling processes.

The second depletion stage targets genomic sequences derived from *Chlorocebus sabaeus*. Vero cell lines are routinely employed for viral propagation and isolation, resulting in substantial host-cell carryover in many sequencing libraries. Removal of Vero-derived reads substantially improves assembly specificity for cultured isolates.

The third depletion stage targets Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) sequences. Although the reference may be replaced by any laboratory-specific contaminant genome, RSV was selected as a representative viral contaminant commonly encountered during respiratory virus sequencing workflows. Reads mapping to the RSV reference are excluded from downstream processing.

The resulting dataset represents a filtered collection of candidate LSDV reads suitable for genome reconstruction.

---

## De Novo Genome Assembly

Filtered reads are assembled using Flye, a long-read assembler specifically designed for error-prone sequencing technologies. Flye employs a repeat graph approach that enables efficient reconstruction of complex genomic structures from long-read sequencing datasets.

Assembly is performed using the high-quality Nanopore mode with an expected genome size of approximately 151 kb, corresponding to the known genome size of LSDV. Flye constructs an assembly graph directly from sequencing read overlaps and generates a draft consensus assembly representing the underlying viral genome.

This assembly stage provides the primary structural reconstruction of the viral genome and serves as the foundation for subsequent polishing and scaffolding procedures.

---

## Consensus Polishing

Draft assemblies generated from Nanopore sequencing data may retain residual substitution, insertion, and deletion errors. To improve consensus accuracy, the workflow performs assembly polishing using Medaka.

Medaka employs neural-network models trained specifically on Oxford Nanopore sequencing data to identify and correct residual assembly errors. Filtered sequencing reads are aligned back to the draft assembly and used to generate an improved consensus sequence.

The resulting polished assembly typically exhibits enhanced nucleotide-level accuracy and reduced error rates relative to the initial assembly generated by Flye.

---

## Reference-Guided Scaffolding

Following consensus polishing, assemblies are scaffolded using RagTag. The scaffolding process utilizes a reference LSDV genome to orient, order, and connect assembled contigs.

Reference-guided scaffolding improves assembly contiguity and facilitates reconstruction of complete viral genomes, particularly in datasets exhibiting local coverage variation or unresolved assembly graph structures. RagTag determines the most likely contig arrangement through alignment against the reference genome while preserving the underlying sequence generated during de novo assembly.

The final scaffolded assembly constitutes the primary genome reconstruction generated by the pipeline.

---

## Alignment and Coverage Analysis

To evaluate assembly completeness and sequencing depth, filtered reads are aligned against the final scaffolded genome using minimap2. Resulting alignments are sorted and indexed using SAMtools.

Genome-wide coverage profiles are subsequently generated using `samtools depth`, producing nucleotide-resolution coverage measurements across the entire assembly. These data facilitate identification of low-coverage regions, coverage dropouts, potential assembly artifacts, and regions requiring additional validation.

Mean genome coverage is calculated from the resulting depth profiles and reported within the final sample summary statistics.

---

## Assembly Quality Assessment

Assembly quality is evaluated using QUAST. Metrics generated during this stage include total assembly size, contig count, N50, GC content, and additional structural statistics describing assembly completeness and continuity.

QUAST outputs provide a standardized framework for comparing assemblies generated from different sequencing runs, samples, or analytical configurations. These metrics support objective assessment of assembly quality and facilitate identification of assemblies requiring further investigation.

---

## Automated Reporting

For each processed sample, the pipeline generates a comprehensive summary containing sequencing statistics, host depletion metrics, assembly characteristics, and coverage measurements. Project-wide metrics are simultaneously aggregated into a tab-delimited summary file to facilitate comparison across multiple samples.

Reported statistics include raw sequencing yield, depletion efficiency, retained read counts, assembly size, contig count, N50, and mean genome coverage. These outputs provide a concise overview of analytical performance and assembly quality across an entire sequencing project.

---

## Computational Reproducibility

The workflow was developed with an emphasis on reproducibility, transparency, and deployment within both standalone and high-performance computing environments. Strict Bash error handling, automated dependency verification, reference validation, and standardized output organization are incorporated throughout the pipeline to ensure robust execution.

All software dependencies are managed through a Conda environment, enabling consistent installation and execution across computational platforms. The workflow is fully compatible with Linux-based workstations, servers, and high-performance computing clusters.
